Gage R&R Calculator

Analyze measurement system repeatability and reproducibility

What is Gage R&R?

Gage R&R (Repeatability and Reproducibility) is a methodology used to evaluate the precision and reliability of a measurement system. It quantifies how much variation in measurements comes from the measurement equipment itself (repeatability) versus how much comes from different operators using the equipment (reproducibility).

A Gage R&R study is essential for:

  1. Determining if a measurement system is adequate for process control
  2. Identifying sources of variation in measurement systems
  3. Qualifying new measurement equipment
  4. Comparing different measurement methods
  5. Meeting quality standards like ISO, IATF, and Six Sigma requirements

    6. The Gage R&R formula calculates the percentage of measurement variation relative to the total process variation, providing a clear metric for evaluating measurement system capability. This methodology is a fundamental component of Measurement System Analysis (MSA) in quality management systems.

    Source: Automotive Industry Action Group (AIAG). (2010). Measurement Systems Analysis (MSA) Manual.

    Gage R&R Formula

    Gage R&R is a statistical tool that measures the amount of variation in the measurement system arising from the measurement device and the operators.

    %GRR = (σ_GRR / σ_Total) × 100

    Where:

    • Repeatability (σ_Repeatability) - Variation from the measurement device (same operator, same part)
      Formula: σ_Repeatability = √(MS_Repeatability), where MS_Repeatability is the mean square for repeatability from ANOVA
    • Reproducibility (σ_Reproducibility) - Variation from different operators (different operators, same part)
      Formula: σ_Reproducibility = √(MS_Reproducibility - MS_Repeatability / (n × r)), where MS_Reproducibility is the mean square for reproducibility, n is the number of parts, and r is the number of trials per operator
    • Total GRR (σ_GRR) - Combined repeatability and reproducibility variation
      Formula: σ_GRR = √(σ_Repeatability² + σ_Reproducibility²)
    • Part Variation (σ_Part) - Variation between different parts being measured
      Formula: σ_Part = √(MS_Part), where MS_Part is the mean square for part variation from ANOVA
    • Total Variation (σ_Total) - Total variation in the measurement system
      Formula: σ_Total = √(σ_GRR² + σ_Part²)

    Example: Gage R&R Study Calculation

    Problem:

    A quality engineer wants to assess the measurement system for a critical dimension. She selects 3 parts, 2 operators, and 2 trials per part. The measurements (in mm) are as follows:

    Part Operator 1
    Trial 1    		 Operator 1
    Trial 2    		 Operator 2
    Trial 1    		 Operator 2
    Trial 2
    1 10.01 10.03 10.00 10.02
    2 10.10 10.12 10.09 10.11
    3 10.20 10.21 10.19 10.20

    Solution Steps:

    1. Calculate average range for each operator:
      Operator 1 ranges: |10.01-10.03|=0.02, |10.10-10.12|=0.02, |10.20-10.21|=0.01
      Average range (Operator 1): (0.02+0.02+0.01)/3 = 0.0167
      Operator 2 ranges: |10.00-10.02|=0.02, |10.09-10.11|=0.02, |10.19-10.20|=0.01
      Average range (Operator 2): (0.02+0.02+0.01)/3 = 0.0167
    2. Overall average range:
      (0.0167 + 0.0167) / 2 = 0.0167
    3. Repeatability (EV):
      For 2 trials, K1 = 4.56 EV = 0.0167 × 4.56 ≈ 0.076

      4. *View K1,K2, K3 values in the glossary section of this page.

    4. Operator averages:
      Operator 1: (10.01+10.03+10.10+10.12+10.20+10.21)/6 = 10.112
      Operator 2: (10.00+10.02+10.09+10.11+10.19+10.20)/6 = 10.102
    5. Reproducibility (AV):
      Difference = |10.112 - 10.102| = 0.01
      For 2 operators, K2 = 3.65
      AV = 0.01 × 3.65 = 0.0365
    6. Total GRR:
      GRR = sqrt(EV² + AV²) = sqrt(0.076² + 0.0365²) ≈ 0.084
    7. Part averages:
      Part 1: (10.01+10.03+10.00+10.02)/4 = 10.015
      Part 2: (10.10+10.12+10.09+10.11)/4 = 10.105
      Part 3: (10.20+10.21+10.19+10.20)/4 = 10.200
    8. Part Variation (PV):
      Range = 10.200 - 10.015 = 0.185
      For 3 parts, K3 = 2.70
      PV = 0.185 × 2.70 = 0.4995
    9. Total Variation (TV):
      TV = sqrt(GRR² + PV²) = sqrt(0.084² + 0.4995²) ≈ 0.506
    10. %GRR:
      %GRR = (0.084 / 0.506) × 100 ≈ 16.6%

    Interpretation:

    Since %GRR is between 10% and 30%, the measurement system may be acceptable depending on application requirements. Further improvement may be considered for critical measurements.

    How to Use The Gage R&R Calculator

    Our Gage R&R calculator simplifies the process of conducting a measurement system analysis. Follow these steps to perform your Gage R&R study:

    1. Study Setup: Enter the number of parts, operators, and trials for your study. Typically, 10 parts, 3 operators, and 3 trials is a standard setup.
    2. Enter Measurements: Input measurement data for each combination of part, operator, and trial in the data table that appears.
    3. Optional Tolerance: If you know the tolerance for the measured characteristic, enter it to calculate percentage of tolerance.
    4. Calculate: Click the "Calculate Gage R&R" button to analyze your measurement system.
    5. Interpret Results: Review the results against standard acceptance criteria to determine if your measurement system is acceptable.

    For accurate results, ensure that measurements are taken randomly and operators are not aware of previous measurements to avoid bias.

    How to Interpret the Results of Gage R&R Calculator

    Understanding the Gage R&R acceptance criteria is essential for interpreting your study results. The Automotive Industry Action Group (AIAG) provides the following guidelines:

    %GRR Interpretation Recommended Action
    < 10% The measurement system is acceptable No action needed. The measurement system has acceptable variation.
    10% - 30% The measurement system may be acceptable based on importance of application, cost of measurement device, cost of repair, etc. Evaluate the risk and cost factors. May be acceptable for some applications.
    > 30% The measurement system needs improvement Identify sources of variation and implement corrective actions.

    Attribute Gage R&R Considerations

    For attribute Gage R&R studies (where measurements are categorical rather than continuous), different acceptance criteria apply:

    • ≥ 90% agreement: Measurement system is acceptable
    • 80% - 90% agreement: May be acceptable based on application risk
    • < 80% agreement: Measurement system needs improvement

      • Attribute studies typically use methods like the hypothesis test analysis or the signal detection method to evaluate measurement system capability for pass/fail or categorical data.

      Source: AIAG. (2010). Measurement Systems Analysis (MSA) Manual, 4th Edition.

      Frequently Asked Questions About Gage R&R

      What is the difference between repeatability and reproducibility? +

      Repeatability refers to the variation in measurements when the same operator measures the same part multiple times using the same measuring instrument. It represents the inherent precision of the measurement equipment.

      Reproducibility refers to the variation in measurements when different operators measure the same part using the same measuring instrument. It represents the consistency between different operators.

      Together, these two components make up the Gage R&R, which quantifies the overall variation attributable to the measurement system itself.

      How many parts, operators, and trials should I use in a Gage R&R study? +

      The AIAG MSA manual recommends:

      • Parts: 10 parts that represent the actual or expected range of process variation
      • Operators: 3 operators who normally perform the measurement
      • Trials: 3 measurements per part by each operator

      This standard setup (10 parts × 3 operators × 3 trials = 90 measurements) provides a robust data set for analysis. However, the specific numbers can be adjusted based on practical constraints while maintaining statistical validity.

      What if my Gage R&R results are above 30%? +

      If your Gage R&R results exceed 30%, your measurement system requires improvement. Consider these corrective actions:

      1. Check for proper calibration of measurement equipment
      2. Provide additional training to operators on measurement procedures
      3. Improve the clarity of measurement instructions and standards
      4. Consider using a more precise measurement instrument
      5. Evaluate the fixture or setup used for measurement
      6. Identify and eliminate sources of environmental variation (temperature, humidity, etc.)

        7. After implementing improvements, conduct another Gage R&R study to verify effectiveness.

        What is the difference between variable and attribute Gage R&R? +

        Variable Gage R&R is used for continuous measurement data (e.g., length, weight, temperature) and analyzes the measurement system using ANOVA or average and range methods.

        Attribute Gage R&R is used for categorical data (e.g., pass/fail, go/no-go, visual inspections) and analyzes the measurement system using hypothesis testing or signal detection methods to evaluate agreement between operators and against known standards.

        The calculator on this page is designed for variable Gage R&R studies. Attribute studies require different methodologies and acceptance criteria.

        How often should Gage R&R studies be performed? +

        Gage R&R studies should be performed:

        1. When introducing new measurement equipment
        2. When there are changes to measurement procedures or operators
        3. After any significant maintenance or repair of measurement equipment
        4. When measurement system problems are suspected
        5. As part of periodic validation (typically annually for critical measurements)
        6. When process changes occur that might affect measurement system performance

          7. Regular Gage R&R studies help ensure that your measurement system remains capable and reliable over time.
