What is Inflation?

Inflation is the rate at which the general level of prices for goods and services rises, eroding purchasing power. As inflation occurs, each unit of currency buys fewer goods and services. This is why the same amount of money buys less today than it did 10, 20, or 30 years ago.

Central banks attempt to limit inflation — and avoid deflation — to keep the economy running smoothly. Moderate inflation is a normal part of economic growth, but high inflation can be problematic for consumers and investors alike.

Why Does Money Lose Value Over Time?

Money loses value over time primarily due to inflation. Several factors contribute to this phenomenon:

  • Increased money supply: When central banks print more money, each unit becomes less valuable
  • Demand-pull inflation: When demand for goods and services exceeds supply, prices rise
  • Cost-push inflation: When production costs increase, businesses pass these costs to consumers
  • Built-in inflation: The expectation of future inflation leads to wage increases, creating a cycle

Understanding why money loses value over time is crucial for financial planning, especially for long-term goals like retirement.

How Inflation Impacts Long-Term Investments

Inflation significantly affects long-term investments and savings. Even moderate inflation can substantially reduce the real value of money over decades. This is why historical prices matter for financial planning and why it's important to adjust for inflation in budgeting.

For example, if you saved $10,000 in 2000, it would only have the purchasing power of about $5,800 in 2000 dollars by 2020, assuming an average inflation rate of 2.5%.

To protect against inflation, investors often allocate portions of their portfolio to assets that tend to outpace inflation, such as stocks, real estate, and inflation-protected securities.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is my money worth after inflation?

Your money's worth after inflation depends on the inflation rate and time period. Use our calculator above to see exactly how much purchasing power your money has lost or gained over specific time periods.

What was the value of a dollar in the past?

The value of a dollar in the past was significantly higher than today. For example, $1 in 1950 had the same purchasing power as about $12 in 2023. This calculator helps you compare historical prices with today's prices.

How much has the cost of living changed over time?

The cost of living has increased dramatically over time due to inflation. Our calculator shows you exactly how much more expensive goods and services have become over any period since 1913.

Why is tracking purchasing power important for retirement planning?

Tracking purchasing power helps with retirement planning because it shows how much income you'll really need in the future. If you don't account for inflation, you might significantly underestimate the amount required to maintain your standard of living in retirement.

What does inflation do to savings over time?

Inflation erodes the value of savings over time. Money kept in low-interest savings accounts may actually lose purchasing power if the interest rate doesn't keep up with inflation. This is why it's important to consider investments that outpace inflation for long-term savings.

Understanding the Consumer Price Index (CPI)

Our calculator uses historical Consumer Price Index (CPI) data from the U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics to measure inflation. The CPI measures the average change over time in the prices paid by urban consumers for a market basket of consumer goods and services.

While CPI is the most common measure of inflation, it's important to remember that it represents an average. Your personal inflation rate might differ based on your spending habits.
