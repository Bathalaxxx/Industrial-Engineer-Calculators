My Warehouse Efficiency Project: A Paired T-Test Success Story

Early in my career, I was tasked with improving operations at a logistics warehouse. The management wanted to implement a new picking process but needed evidence that it would actually reduce processing time. We conducted a time and motion study where the same warehouse picker used both the current and proposed methods with the same equipment.

This created the perfect scenario for a paired t-test - we had before-and-after measurements from the same subject under different conditions. The results showed a statistically significant reduction in processing time, which gave management the confidence to implement the new process across all operations. The project ultimately reduced picking time by 23% and increased overall warehouse throughput.

What is a Paired T-Test?

A paired t-test (also called a dependent samples t-test) is a statistical procedure used to determine whether the mean difference between two sets of observations is zero. In a paired design, each subject is measured twice, resulting in pairs of observations.

t = (M - μ) / (s / √n)

Where:

  • M = Mean of the differences between paired observations
  • μ = Hypothesized mean difference (usually 0)
  • s = Standard deviation of the differences
  • n = Number of pairs

When to Use a Paired T-Test

Paired t-tests are appropriate in these common scenarios:

  • Before-and-after studies: Measuring the effect of an intervention or treatment
  • Matched pairs: When subjects are paired based on specific characteristics
  • Repeated measures: When the same subject is measured under different conditions
  • Quality control: Comparing two measurement methods on the same items

Paired vs. Unpaired T-Tests: Key Differences

Aspect Paired T-Test Unpaired T-Test
Design Same subjects measured twice Different groups of subjects
Data Structure Dependent samples Independent samples
Variance Lower (controls for individual differences) Higher (includes individual differences)
Statistical Power Higher (more sensitive to detect differences) Lower
When to Use Before-after studies, matched pairs Comparing two separate groups

Common Mistakes to Avoid with Paired T-Tests

Based on my experience, here are the most common pitfalls when conducting paired t-tests:

  1. Using independent tests for paired data: This reduces statistical power and can lead to incorrect conclusions.
  2. Not checking assumptions: Paired t-tests assume that the differences are normally distributed, especially with small sample sizes.
  3. Ignoring the order effect: In before-after studies, the order of treatments might influence results.
  4. Small sample sizes: With fewer than 10 pairs, the test may not have enough power to detect meaningful differences.
  5. Not reporting confidence intervals: Always include confidence intervals for the mean difference, not just the p-value.
